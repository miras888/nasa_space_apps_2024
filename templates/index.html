Mars Seismic Detector

Mars experiences seismic activity, or "marsquakes," primarily due to three causes: internal cooling and contraction of the planet's crust, tectonic fractures, and meteorite impacts. Unlike Earth, Mars lacks active tectonic plates, so its quakes result from the gradual release of internal heat and surface stresses. NASA's InSight mission has detected thousands of marsquakes using its SEIS instrument. These quakes vary in size and depth, providing valuable data about Mars’ interior, such as the thickness of its crust and properties of the mantle and core. Studying Mars' seismic activity offers insights into the planet’s geologic evolution and the processes shaping rocky planets.

Moon Seismic Survey

The Moon experiences seismic activity known as "moonquakes," which differ from Earth's earthquakes in both cause and intensity. There are four types of moonquakes: deep, shallow, thermal, and those caused by meteorite impacts. Deep moonquakes are caused by tidal forces exerted by Earth's gravity and occur hundreds of kilometers below the surface. Shallow moonquakes are the most powerful and can last up to 10 minutes, but their exact cause is unclear, potentially linked to the Moon's cooling and contraction. Thermal moonquakes happen when the Moon's surface expands as it heats up during lunar day and contracts when it cools at night. Impact moonquakes result from meteorites striking the lunar surface. NASA's Apollo missions in the 1970s deployed seismometers that recorded these moonquakes. While less frequent and intense than earthquakes, moonquakes provide key insights into the Moon’s interior structure, such as its crust, mantle, and core composition. Understanding seismic activity on the Moon is crucial for future lunar exploration and potential habitation, as it helps assess surface stability and geologic activity.

About seismic problems

Transmitting seismic data from Mars is power-intensive, and much of the data is not scientifically useful. Most of the time, only a small fraction of this data contains valuable signals. By processing the data directly on the lander to filter out noise, we can prioritize sending only meaningful information. This saves power, reduces data transmission needs, and makes it easier for researchers to access the important seismic data they need for planetary exploration.
